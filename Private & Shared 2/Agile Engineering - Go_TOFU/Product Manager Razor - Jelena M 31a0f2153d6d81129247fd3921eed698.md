# Interview Summary – Jelena Kurtovic (Razer)

**Date:** March 04, 2026 **Conducted by:** Emanuele Scammacca del Murgo & Fabian Thiele (CODE University of Applied Sciences) **Duration:** ~30 minutes **Context:** Customer discovery interview for a university startup project aimed at building an AI tool for product managers.

---

## About the Interviewee

Jelena Kurtovic is a product manager with experience across multiple companies and industries. Her roles have included positions at **CertiSat** (GPS/fleet management), **Meister** (SaaS productivity tools, competitor to Notion and Miro), and most recently **Razer**, where she worked in an **Operational Intelligence** function. Her background spans both external-client-facing and internal-client-facing product management.

---

## Team & Organizational Structure at Razer

At Razer, Jelena worked within an operational intelligence setup. Her immediate team consisted of developers at varying experience levels, each with adapted sprint cadences:

- **Senior/fast developer** – scrums every two days
- **Mid-level developer** – scrums every week and a half
- **Junior developer** – longer scrums with additional mentoring and oversight

She worked closely with **internal stakeholders as clients**, most notably a Head of Compliance, rather than external customers. She interfaced regularly with commercial leads, and emphasized that the **relationship between product and commercial is critical** — when that relationship breaks down, the company suffers.

---

## Workflow & Methodology

Jelena uses an **Agile/Kanban-based approach**, working with epics, user stories, and tasks. Key practices include:

- **Biweekly retros** to review progress and define the next development phase
- **Acceptance criteria** to define "done" for each feature
- **Kanban boards** (primarily in Jira) to manage daily developer tasks
- **Time estimates in hours/days** rather than story points, with built-in buffer when communicating timelines to commercial stakeholders
- **User interviews** using the *Jobs To Be Done* framework, combined with behavioral data tools such as **Amplitude** and a shadowing/heatmap tool (name to be confirmed) to understand user behavior on the product

---

## Identified Pain Points

### ✅ Real Pain Points

- **The "how", not the "what"** — Knowing *what* to build is rarely the problem. Determining *how* to build it, especially for complex features involving many stakeholders, is where friction arises. One project (PIM) required 17 stakeholders and still struggled to reach completion.
- **Undefined or incomplete processes** — The most significant recurring blocker. If the underlying business process is not properly documented and clean, automation or product development cannot proceed. Jelena spent time on process mapping before actual product work could begin. The best client she ever had (Head of Compliance at Razer) had spotless, fully documented processes — enabling two products to be delivered in under two weeks.
- **Time estimation** — Developer estimates are consistently unreliable. Jelena's workaround is to apply a personal buffer (one-third to one-half extra) when communicating timelines externally, without surfacing this in the Kanban board.
- **Stakeholder and user management at scale** — When a feature or product involves a large number of users or stakeholders with conflicting needs, prioritization and alignment become extremely painful.
- **Explaining new technology to non-technical users** — Particularly in client-facing roles (e.g., CertiSat), users often cannot articulate what they need in technical terms and may resist solutions (e.g., preferring Excel over predictive algorithms), requiring significant negotiation and education effort.

### ❌ Not a Pain Point

- **Knowing what to build next** — Jelena was explicit: *"If the what is a problem, you have a problem in your role."* Feedback from clients, internal stakeholders, or market signals consistently makes the next priority clear.
- **Tooling for day-to-day task management** — Jelena expressed comfort and familiarity with Jira and Kanban. She would be reluctant to migrate to a new tool mid-project, citing switching costs and team disruption.
- **Data aggregation for established products** — Tools like Amplitude and BI dashboards already address this well. She does not see a strong gap here for an AI tool to fill.

---

## Potential Opportunity Area

Jelena pointed to **growth product management for market expansion** as an underserved area. A tool that aggregates retention metrics, user behavior data, and market intelligence (competitors, investment trends) in a pre-structured, Reforge-aligned framework — without requiring heavy customization — could be genuinely valuable. She noted this would be most useful for **established products entering new markets**, not early-stage products with no user base.
