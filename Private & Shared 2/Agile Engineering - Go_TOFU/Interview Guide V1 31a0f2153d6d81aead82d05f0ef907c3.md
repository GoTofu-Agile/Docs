# Interview Guide V1

# Product Discovery AI — Exploratory Interview Guide (Final)

***Target duration:** **~25 minutes***

***Target interviewee:** Product Managers, Product Leads, Founders doing PM work*

***Methodology:** The Mom Test — ask about their life, not your idea. No pitching until Part 7. Learn from specifics in the past, not opinions about the future.*

---

## Interviewer Guidelines

- Never describe your product idea before Part 7. You're here to learn, not to pitch.
- Ask about what they actually do today, not what they would do hypothetically.
- Anchor every question in specific, real past events ("Tell me about the last time...").
- When they say something is "painful" or "annoying," dig into specifics: frequency, time cost, consequences.
- Compliments and enthusiasm are noise. Commitments and facts are signal.
- If they haven't tried to solve the problem themselves, it probably isn't a real problem for them.
- Listen for workarounds — they reveal real pain better than stated preferences.
- **You're exploring broadly first.** Don't assume "what to build next" is the hardest problem. Let them tell you what is.
- **This interview is a workflow audit disguised as a conversation.** You want to leave with a mental movie of their week.

---

## Part 1 — Who Are You, Really? (3 min)

*Goal: Build a detailed profile. We need to know the type of product, the org, the PM's actual scope — not their LinkedIn title.*

**Q1. Can you tell me about your current role? What's the product you work on, and who are your users?**

*Intent: Classify — digital vs. physical, B2B vs. B2C, who is the end user vs. buyer?*

- Is your product primarily software, or does it have physical/hardware components?
- Who actually uses it day-to-day — and are they the ones who bought it?

**Q2. What does your immediate team look like, and where does product management sit in the org?***Intent: Org size, stage, autonomy. A PM at a 15-person startup lives a completely different life from one in a 5,000-person enterprise.*

- How many PMs are there? How many engineers per PM?
- Do you have dedicated designers, researchers, data analysts — or do you wear those hats?
- Who do you report to — and who has final say on what gets built?

---

## Part 2 — A Day and a Week in Your Life (6 min)

*Goal: Build a granular, hour-by-hour picture of what this person actually does. You want to leave this section able to describe their week better than they can.*

**Q3. Walk me through yesterday — or the last "normal" workday you had. From when you opened your laptop to when you closed it, what did you actually do?***Intent: Get the raw, unfiltered sequence. Don't let them give you the idealized version. Push for specifics.*

- What was the first thing you looked at?
- What meetings did you have, and what were they about?
- What did you produce or write?
- What decisions did you make — even small ones?
- When did you feel most productive? When did you feel like you were spinning your wheels?

**Q4. Now zoom out to the last full week. What are the recurring rituals, meetings, and tasks that always happen?** *Intent: Map the repeating structure — standups, sprint planning, roadmap reviews, customer calls, 1:1s, stakeholder updates. Understand what's scheduled vs. what's reactive.*

- What's on your calendar every single week without fail?
- How much of your week is scheduled vs. "I'll figure it out as I go"?
- What tasks keep coming back that you wish you could do once and be done with?

**Q5. Who are all the people you interact with in a typical week, and what do those interactions look like?***Intent: Map the human network — engineering, design, data, sales, support, leadership, customers. Understand what each interaction is about and what triggers it.*

- For each person/group: What do you typically need from them, or they from you?
- Which of those interactions are smooth, and which ones are friction-heavy?
- Are there people you should be talking to more but don't have time for?

**Q6. What are the actual deliverables you're responsible for — the things people expect from you?***Intent: Full output inventory. PRDs, roadmaps, sprint tickets, competitive analyses, board updates, go/no-go decisions, pricing recommendations — whatever it is.*

- Which of those takes the most time to create?
- Which one has the most impact on what actually happens?
- Is there anything you're expected to deliver that feels like busywork?
- Is there something you wish you could deliver but never have time for?

---

## Part 3 — Signals, Triggers & Decision Points (5 min)

*Goal: Understand the specific inputs and cues that drive their actions. Get granular — tool names, data types, frequency, and the decision trees in their head.*

**Q7. Walk me through the last time you noticed something — a data point, a customer comment, a market shift — that made you change course or take action.***Intent: Understand triggers. What specifically caused them to shift? A metric dropping, a churned customer, a competitor launch, a support ticket spike, a Slack message from sales?*

- What was the signal, and where did you see it?
- What did you do next — step by step?
- How did you decide it was important enough to act on vs. just noise?
- How long from "I noticed this" to "the team is working on it"?

**Q8. What usage data or product analytics do you look at, and how?***Intent: Get specific tool names, metrics, and frequency. Are they in Amplitude, Mixpanel, Pendo, Heap, Looker, custom dashboards, raw SQL? Daily, weekly, ad hoc?*

- What tools do you use to look at product data?
- What are the 2–3 metrics you check most often, and why those?
- How comfortable are you pulling data yourself vs. relying on a data team?
- Is there data you wish you had but don't?
- When you look at a dashboard, what are you actually looking for — what triggers an "uh oh" or an "interesting"?

**Q9. Beyond analytics, what other sources of input do you rely on to understand what's happening with users?***Intent: Map the full signal landscape — support tickets, sales call recordings, NPS, user interviews, session replays, Slack channels, social media, win/loss. Structured vs. unstructured, proactive vs. reactive.*

- How do you collect qualitative feedback — calls, surveys, support, something else?
- How do you keep track of it over time? Is it in a spreadsheet, a tool, your head?
- What's the most valuable source of insight you have — and what makes it valuable?
- What input do you feel you're missing or underweighting?

**Q10. When you have conflicting signals — users say one thing, data shows another, leadership wants a third — how do you decide?***Intent: Uncover the decision tree. Is there a framework, or is it gut? What do they weigh most heavily?*

- Can you give me a specific example of when signals conflicted?
- What did you do, and how did it turn out?
- Is there a hierarchy in your head — like "data beats opinion" or "biggest customer wins"?

---

## Part 4 — Pain, Friction & What Breaks (5 min)

*Goal: This is the core validation section. Dig deep into inefficiencies, frustrations, and broken processes. You want them to feel the pain as they describe it.*

**Q11. If I followed you around for a week with a stopwatch, what would I catch you spending time on that you'd be embarrassed about — things that feel like they shouldn't take as long as they do?***Intent: Surface hidden time sinks. The embarrassment framing gets past the "everything is fine" instinct.*

- Why does it take so long?
- Have you tried to speed it up? What happened?

**Q12. Think about the last time you felt genuinely frustrated at work — not with a person, but with a process or a tool. What was happening?***Intent: Emotional anchor. Frustration = real pain. Get the story, then quantify it.*

- How often does that happen — once a month, once a week, every day?
- What do you do when it happens — power through, find a workaround, complain and move on?
- What does it cost you — in time, quality, or energy?

**Q13. What's a decision you've had to make recently where you felt like you were flying blind — you didn't have enough information, or the information was scattered, and you just had to make a call?***Intent: Surfaces information gaps and synthesis failures. If this is common, it validates the core thesis.*

- What information would have helped?
- Where would it have come from?
- How did the decision turn out?

**Q14. Have you ever built something that turned out to be the wrong thing — or the right thing but done wrong? Walk me through what happened.***Intent: Failure stories reveal the real cost. Probe for root cause — bad signal, bad synthesis, bad execution, or misalignment?*

- When did you realize it was wrong?
- What would have helped you catch it earlier?
- What did it cost — in time, trust, revenue, morale?

**Q15. Of everything we've talked about — the time sinks, the frustrations, the flying-blind moments, the things that break — if you could only fix ONE, which one would transform how you work?***Intent: Force-rank the pain. Compare to your hypothesis.*

- Why that one over the others?
- How are you dealing with it today?
- What have you tried? What did you spend on it?

**Q16. And what's the second one?***Intent: The first answer is sometimes the "safe" answer. The second often reveals the deeper, more personal pain.*

**Q17. Is there something about your job that you think is fundamentally broken — not just slow or annoying, but structurally wrong — that most people just accept as "how it is"?***Intent: Go for the systemic insight. This question often surfaces beliefs about the role itself that point to big opportunities.*

- Why do you think it's still this way?
- Have you seen anyone do it differently?

---

## Part 5 — Tools & AI (2 min)

*Goal: Quick audit of current stack and organic AI adoption.*

**Q18. If you listed every tool you touch in a typical week for product work, what's on the list?***Intent: Full tool inventory. Listen for fragmentation — 8+ tools that don't connect.*

- Which do you love, which do you tolerate, which do you hate?
- Is there a gap — something you wish existed?

**Q19. Have you started using AI — ChatGPT, Copilot, anything — for any part of your product work?***Intent: Understand organic adoption without suggesting use cases.*

- What do you use it for?
- What works and what doesn't?
- Have you tried it for something and stopped? Why?

---

## Part 6 — The Open Door (1 min)

**Q20. Is there anything about how you work — the hard parts, the weird parts, the parts you've never told anyone about — that I should have asked about but didn't?***Intent: Give permission for the real talk. This is often where the best insight lives.*

---

## Part 7 — Solution Exploration (3 min)

*Goal: Now — and only now — softly introduce the concept. You've gathered unbiased data. Anchor the pitch in THEIR words and pain points from earlier. Watch for polite enthusiasm vs. genuine pull.*

*Transition language: "Thanks for sharing all of that — it's super helpful. I want to run something by you based on what I'm hearing, and I'd love your honest gut reaction."*

**Q21. Restate their #1 pain in their own words. We're exploring the idea of a tool that takes the inputs you already have — customer interviews, support tickets, usage data — and helps you synthesize them into a clear picture of what's worth building and why. Not a dashboard. More like an AI collaborator for the messy middle of product work. What's your immediate, honest reaction?***Intent: Watch their body language and first three words. "That's interesting" = polite noise. "Wait, would it also..." or "How would it handle..." = genuine pull. Silence followed by a specific question = gold.*

- What part of that sounds useful, if any?
- What part sounds like BS, or like it wouldn't actually work?
- Does it connect to the problems you described, or does it feel like a solution to a different problem?

**Q22. If a tool like that existed and worked well — where would it rank in your stack of problems? Is this a "nice to have, maybe someday" or a "I would clear my afternoon to try this right now"?***Intent: Calibrate urgency. Their ranking matters more than their enthusiasm.*

- What would it need to do on day one to be worth your time?
- What's the single thing it would need to nail for you to trust it?
- What would make you not use it, even if it worked?

**Q23. Imagine it's been three months and you're using this every week. What changed about your work? What are you doing differently?***Intent: Let them paint the picture. If they can't articulate what changes, the value prop isn't landing. If they immediately describe a specific scenario — that's your marketing copy.*

- What did it replace — a tool, a meeting, a manual process?
- Who else on your team would care about this?

**Q24. If we built a rough version of this in the next few weeks, would you be willing to try it on a real problem — not as a favor, but because it might actually help?***Intent: The ultimate Mom Test commitment question. "Sure, send me an email" = no. "Can I bring my team lead into the next call?" = yes. Anything that costs them real time or reputation = genuine signal.*

- What would that look like practically?
- What data or context would you need to give us to make it useful?
- Is there a specific decision coming up where you'd want to test it?

*Interviewer note: Pay close attention to the gap between what they said in Parts 2–5 (unbiased pain) and how they react in Part 7. If the solution doesn't map to their stated pain — that's your most important learning. If they start solving your design problems for you ("yeah but it would need to also do X") — you're onto something.*
